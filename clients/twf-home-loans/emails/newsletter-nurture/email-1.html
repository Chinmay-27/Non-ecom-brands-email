3 signs you're overpaying
your mortgage right now

Most homeowners are quietly losing hundreds of dollars every month — and have no idea. Here's how to tell if you're one of them.

Hi {{first_name}},

Here's a question most homeowners never ask themselves: Am I actually paying what I should be for my home loan — or am I just paying what I've always paid?

In 35 years of helping California homeowners, I've seen it over and over again. Good, hardworking people — paying faithfully every month — who are quietly sending their lender $200, $300, even $500 more than they need to. Not because they're irresponsible. Because no one ever sat down and showed them a better option.

Below are the three most common signs I see. Read them honestly, and if any of them sound familiar, I'd like to show you what's actually possible — at no cost to you.
 
1

You're carrying high-interest credit card debt

Credit card interest rates often run 19% to 29%. Your home loan interest rate is almost certainly a fraction of that — and in many cases, that interest may be tax-deductible.

By rolling high-interest card balances into a home loan, many of my clients cut their total monthly debt payment significantly — even when they thought they had no equity to tap. We have programs specifically designed for that situation, with no appraisal required in most cases and loans from $15,000 to $500,000.
2

Your rate is meaningfully higher than today's options

If you closed your loan a few years ago — or if you accepted a higher rate because your credit wasn't perfect at the time — it may be worth checking what you'd qualify for today.

A refinance doesn't always require perfect credit or a mountain of equity. We work with impaired credit, non-traditional income, self-employed borrowers, and even homeowners who've been through bankruptcy. The banks that turned you down don't represent all your options — not by a long shot.
3

Your monthly breathing room keeps shrinking

When mortgage, car payments, credit cards, and everyday expenses eat up nearly every dollar you bring in, it doesn't always mean you need to earn more. Sometimes it means your debt is structured in the most expensive way possible.

One of my clients, John from Irvine, described what we did as "getting a raise without getting a raise." He and his wife Lisa saved $475 a month — not by changing their lifestyle, but by restructuring what they already owed. That's real money back in your pocket every single month.
