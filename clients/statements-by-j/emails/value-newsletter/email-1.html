This Month's Design Tip

How to make a small living room feel twice as big — without moving a single wall.

The one rule most people miss: in a tight room, scale matters more than size. A single large sofa with clean lines will always read bigger than three small pieces crammed together. Fewer, bolder pieces. That's the whole trick.

Here's how designers actually do it in small spaces:

01

Float the furniture off the walls. Pushing everything to the perimeter makes the room feel like a waiting room. Pull the sofa 6–12 inches in and anchor it with a rug. Suddenly the space breathes.

02

Choose one statement piece, then calm everything else. A bold accent chair or a sculptural console reads as intentional. If everything is competing for attention, nothing wins and the room feels cluttered even when it isn't.

03

Go vertical. Low-profile furniture makes ceilings feel higher. A tall, narrow bookcase or floor lamp draws the eye up. Combined with slightly taller-than-you-think curtains hung close to the ceiling, it's the visual lift the room needs.

04

Use a large rug, not a small one. The instinct is to scale down in a small space. Don't. A rug that's too small makes the furniture look like it's floating on a postage stamp. Go bigger than you think — at minimum, all front legs on the rug.

These are the actual principles designers use whether the room is 400 sq ft or 4,000. The only difference is the price tag — and that's where we come in.
