3 things that make a banner actually get noticed

1

Design for 20 feet, not for your laptop screen

People read a sidewalk banner from across a parking lot, not from six inches away. Your headline should be the single largest element — ideally readable in under two seconds at full stride. Anything smaller than 3 inches of letter height at full print size will disappear. A good rule of thumb: if you have to squint at the proof on your screen, it will be invisible on the street.
2

Contrast beats color every time

Dark text on a light background — or light text on a dark background — is the single highest-leverage change you can make. Red on black, white on navy, yellow on dark green. The moment you put navy blue text on a medium blue background, you've lost the reader before they slow down. If you're not sure, squint at the design: if the text goes fuzzy, the contrast isn't high enough.
3

One message. Not four.

Banners fail when the designer tries to pack in a logo, a tagline, a phone number, a QR code, a list of services, and a "Grand Opening" burst. A banner is not a brochure. Pick the one thing you want someone to remember — the business name, the event, the offer — and let everything else be quiet support. The best banners we've printed look almost too simple on screen. On the street, they're impossible to miss.

File spec quick note

Sending us a file for a large-format banner? Set your resolution to 100–150 DPI at full print size (not 72 DPI screen resolution). A 3×6 ft banner file at 72 DPI prints blurry every time. If you're unsure, just send us what you have — we'll review the file and tell you exactly what's needed before anything goes to press.
