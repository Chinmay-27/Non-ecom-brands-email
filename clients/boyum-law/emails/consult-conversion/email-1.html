When an online will genuinely is enough

If your situation is straightforward — you're single or married with no minor children, your assets are modest, retirement accounts and life insurance already have beneficiaries named, and you own no real property in your own name — a simple online will template, properly signed and witnessed, can do the job. I'd rather you have a basic document than nothing at all.

If that's your situation, LegalZoom might genuinely be fine. I'm serious.

But here's where a $99 template quietly costs your heirs far more

The problem I see over and over in my office isn't that families used an online tool. It's that the document wasn't executed correctly under Nebraska law — or it was, but the trust was never funded, so the home still went through probate anyway.

Here's what that looks like in practice: a couple spends $500 on a revocable living trust from an online service. They assume the house is now protected. But they never re-titled the deed into the name of the trust — a step most templates don't walk you through. When one spouse passes, the house isn't in the trust. It goes through Nebraska probate. Their children wait 9–14 months and spend thousands in court costs and attorney fees to get what the parents intended to leave them.

"I don't want my kids to lose the house to probate costs." — I hear this every week. The goal is right. The risk is in the details: a template that isn't properly executed or funded under Nebraska law can send the house through probate anyway, regardless of what the document says.

This isn't a knock on online services. It's a knock on the assumption that signing the document is the finish line. In Nebraska, it usually isn't.

Add minor children, a family farm, a small business, a blended family, or a child with special needs — and the gap between "template" and "plan that actually works" grows quickly.
