01 — Destination Spotlight

The Azores: Europe’s best-kept secret — and the window is closing

Nine volcanic islands rising out of the mid-Atlantic. Crater lakes that look like something out of a geography textbook dream. Whale watching so consistent it borders on unfair. And — for now — a fraction of the crowds you’d fight in the Canaries or Madeira.

The Azores are having a moment. Travel press has been building buzz for two years, but the infrastructure is still small-island friendly — guesthouses where the owner cooks your breakfast, coastal trails with zero foot traffic at 9am, and flights from the US East Coast under six hours. That combination has a shelf life.

Why now: June through September is the sweet spot — the infamous Atlantic wind calms down, whale season runs through mid-July, and the crowds that flood mainland Portugal haven’t arrived in volume yet. Book a trip for August and you’ll still feel like you discovered the place yourself.

The islands worth knowing:

São Miguel — The big island. Furnas Valley, twin crater lakes at Sete Cidades, and hot-spring-stewed pork. Your base camp.
Flores — The far west. Waterfalls, isolation, and the wildest landscape in the archipelago. Worth the tiny prop plane.
Pico — A 7,713-foot volcano that you can summit overnight. Serious hikers only, but it’s the kind of sunrise that rewires your brain.

02 — The Practical Bit

Stop over-packing for adventure trips (and what to do instead)

The single most common mistake on adventure trips isn’t a bad itinerary — it’s a bag that weighs 28 pounds when it only needs to weigh 14. Here’s the framework we give every traveler we plan for:

The 3-column test. Before you pack anything, draw three columns: Carry Every Day, Use Once, and Just In Case. Everything in the “Just In Case” column stays home. Full stop.

The real weight culprits: extra shoes (bring two pairs, maximum), redundant layers (one rain jacket does everything), and “what if I go somewhere nice” clothing (you won’t, or the adventure version of nice is fine).

The carry-on rule: If you can’t carry it on the plane, you’ve packed too much. Checked luggage on adventure trips means you spend the first 45 minutes of every morning playing Tetris with your bag instead of exploring.

“I’d been putting off Patagonia for eleven years”

“

I’d been saying ‘someday, Patagonia’ since 2015. I had seventeen tabs open at any given moment — different trekking companies, conflicting advice about whether W or O was the right circuit, blog posts from 2019 that may or may not have been accurate. I was so deep in research I was paralyzed by it.

We got on a thirty-minute call and within a week I had a real itinerary — not a list of options, a plan. Eight days, the W Circuit, a night in a refugio that I never would have found on my own. The flight was the only thing I had to think about.

Day four, coming off the Valle del Francés with the Paine towers behind me, I genuinely couldn’t believe it had taken me this long. But I was also glad it hadn’t happened until someone else handled the logistics.”

3 trips with good availability before the year’s out

If you’ve been meaning to make something happen in 2026, here’s what still has room — and why each one rewards going sooner rather than later:
1
 Iceland in September — Northern lights season starts and the summer crowds are already gone. Shoulder-season pricing, near-empty roads. Best month of the year if you ask us.
2
 Peru (non-Machu Picchu) — The Salkantay Trek and Huayna Picchu have no lottery system and near-identical scenery. October is dry-season tail-end and you’re not competing with peak-season crowds.
3
 Vietnam end-to-end — November through December is the sweet spot between monsoon seasons, and the country rewards a slow north-to-south (or reverse) pace over two weeks. Flights are filling faster than usual this year.
