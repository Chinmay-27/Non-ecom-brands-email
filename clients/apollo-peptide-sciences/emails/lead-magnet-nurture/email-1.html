How to read a peptide COA — and spot the vendors hiding one

Most suppliers mention purity. Almost none prove it. Here's what a real Certificate of Analysis contains — and the three numbers that separate a trustworthy vendor from one hiding behind a label.

Hi {{first_name}},

In a space where every vendor claims ≥98% purity, the ones worth sourcing from can prove it with a verifiable paper trail. Most can't — or won't.

A Certificate of Analysis is the primary instrument of vendor accountability in research-grade sourcing. Understanding what it must contain — and what it's missing when a vendor buries it — is one of the more practical skills you can bring to procurement decisions. Let's walk through the anatomy.

The anatomy of a real COA

Number 1

HPLC Purity %

High-Performance Liquid Chromatography separates a compound from impurities using a solvent-column interaction. The chromatogram integrates to a purity percentage — the target peak's area as a fraction of total peak area. Research-grade requires ≥98%. Any lower and impurity variables compound across experimental conditions and threaten reproducibility. The COA should report the exact measured figure for that specific batch, not a site-wide floor claim in a product description.

Number 2

Mass-Spec Identity Confirmation

Mass spectrometry (ESI-MS or MALDI) confirms structural identity by comparing the measured molecular mass against the theoretical mass of the peptide sequence. This is not redundant with HPLC — it answers a different question. A high HPLC purity without mass-spec confirmation means you know the compound is mostly pure, but not that it's purely the right compound. Both numbers together close the identity loop.

Number 3

The Issuing Independent Lab

A COA issued by the vendor's internal team is not third-party verification — it's a vendor making a claim about their own product. An independent analytical laboratory with its own accreditation is what converts a result from an assertion into evidence. The lab's name, address, and analyst signature should appear on the document. If they don't, the document is not a third-party COA regardless of what it's called.

What the hiding looks like

Vendors who can't pass the three-number test have a playbook for not showing it:

→ "COA available upon request" — meaning it may not exist until the request creates sufficient pressure to generate one
→ "Tested to ≥98% purity" as a site-wide claim, applied to every SKU, with no batch-level document to back it
→ A COA with HPLC purity only, no mass-spec identity — one number without the other tells you nothing about what compound you're actually measuring
→ A laboratory name on the document that resolves to nothing — no website, no accreditation listing, no verifiable existence outside the PDF
