This Month's Idea

The 4% problem: what actually lives in the impurity fraction

A peptide reported at 95% HPLC purity is routinely treated as acceptable. In many contexts it is. But for reproducible in-vitro work — where you're attributing a cellular response to a defined compound — that remaining 5% is a variable you cannot control for, and often cannot identify.

What's in that fraction? Depending on the synthesis route and downstream purification, impurities in commercial peptide preparations typically include:

Truncated sequences — incomplete assembly products that may bind your target receptor with altered affinity, producing off-target signal that looks like biology.

Deletion sequences — single amino-acid omissions that shift secondary structure and can behave as partial agonists or silent antagonists in your assay.

Oxidation and deamidation products — degradation artifacts that accumulate when storage conditions drift and confound dose-response curves batch-to-batch.

At 95% purity, you have unknowns representing one molecule in twenty in your working solution. When you're characterising a dose-response curve or establishing an EC₅₀, that noise compounds — especially across multiple batches where the impurity profile may shift entirely even if the headline purity number doesn't.

The compound math

Run two compounds at 95% purity in a co-treatment assay: the probability that any given experimental molecule pair is fully correct is 0.95 × 0.95 = 0.90. You're operating with a 10% noise floor before you touch a pipette — and neither compound's COA flags it.

Stepping up to ≥99% purity doesn't eliminate variance in your assay — but it removes compound identity as a confounding variable. When a result diverges between experimental runs, you investigate assay conditions, protocol drift, or cell passage number. Not whether your input material was what the label said.

That's the practical case for ≥99% HPLC verification. Not theoretical precision — attribution. The ability to state with confidence: the compound is not the source of variance.
