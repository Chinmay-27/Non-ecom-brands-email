The idle-weeks problem

Why owning 1/6 of a yacht beats owning the whole thing

A quick thought on the math most yacht brokers never show you.

Hi {{first_name}},

Somewhere in a marina right now, a gorgeous 65-foot catamaran is sitting on the dock in perfect condition — crew on standby, diesel topped up, fridge full. Its owner is in a boardroom in New York.

That boat will sit there, on average, for 47 weeks this year. And the owner will still write checks for every one of them.

I think about this a lot. Not as an abstract statistic — as a structural problem that fractional ownership was built to solve. Here’s the math, laid out plainly.

The Real Numbers

Sole ownership vs. a 1/6 share: what you actually pay per week on the water

Take a representative luxury catamaran — let’s call it a mid-fleet 65-footer at roughly $3.5 million fully equipped. Here’s how the annual economics compare (figures are illustrative estimates; your specific vessel and share will vary):

Cost item Sole owner 1/6 share
Vessel acquisition ~$3.5M+ ~$575K
Annual crew & ops (est.) $180K–280K Your share
Charter income offset (projected) You manage it Bundled in
Weeks you actually use ~5 (if lucky) Up to 5 — guaranteed

Figures are illustrative estimates based on typical market data. Actual costs, charter income, and availability vary by vessel and season. Investment return claims are not guaranteed.

The math changes the conversation. A sole owner pays the full freight for 52 weeks to use five of them. A co-owner pays a proportionate share — and the remaining 47 weeks become charter inventory that works for you, not a liability sitting at anchor.

The weeks you actually want on the water? You still get them. The weeks you’d be paying for while sitting in an office? Those are someone else’s charter, generating income that historically offsets a meaningful portion of your annual costs. Not a guarantee — but a structural advantage sole ownership simply can’t replicate.

 

This Season’s Anchorage

The Ionian Islands, Greece — July through September

37° N, 20° E

The Ionian Sea

Lefkada  •  Kefalonia  •  Ithaca  •  Zakynthos

If you’ve sailed the Med before, you know the Ionian is something different. Less traffic than the Aegean. More consistent breeze (the meltemi rarely reaches here). Villages that still feel like villages. And anchorages — hidden coves backed by limestone cliffs — where you can be completely alone on a Tuesday in August.

Our Mediterranean fleet will be based primarily in Corfu and Lefkada this summer, with a crossing to Kefalonia and Ithaca pencilled into most custom itineraries. The sailing is approachable — day passages of 20–40 miles, deep-water anchorages with good holding, and a Michelin-recommended taverna in Fiskardo that our crew knows by name.

One More Thought

The question I get most often

“What if I can’t get the weeks I want?”

It’s a fair question, and I’d be skeptical of anyone who dismissed it. Here’s the honest answer: co-owner scheduling is structured, not a free-for-all. Weeks are allocated by rotating priority so no single owner is perpetually locked out of peak season. And because our fleet migrates — Mediterranean in summer, Caribbean in winter — there are two distinct peak seasons per year, doubling the premium window.

The owners who are happiest are the ones who wanted flexibility anyway — a week in June, maybe two in February in the BVI. That’s exactly what the model is designed to deliver.
