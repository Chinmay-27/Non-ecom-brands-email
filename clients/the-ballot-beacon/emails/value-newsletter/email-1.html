What the SAVE Act actually means for your voting rights

Hey{{first_name ? ', ' + first_name : ''}} — a federal voting-eligibility law just cleared Congress and it changes what you’ll need to bring to the polls. Here’s exactly what it says, why it passed, and what you’ll need to do before your next election.

⏱  4-minute read. Three things to know, one action if it applies to you.
1 What passed

The SAVE Act requires proof of citizenship to register to vote in federal elections.

Congress passed the Safeguard American Voter Eligibility (SAVE) Act, and it was signed into law. The law amends the National Voter Registration Act to require documentary proof of U.S. citizenship when registering to vote in any federal election — president, U.S. Senate, or U.S. House.

Acceptable documents include a U.S. passport, a birth certificate, or a Real ID-compliant state driver’s license marked with a citizenship indicator. The law tasks states with setting up the exact verification procedures; federal agency databases (USCIS and SSA records) will be used for cross-checking where available.

Existing registered voters are not affected. The new requirement applies at the point of new registration or re-registration (e.g., after moving states).

2 Why it matters

It changes voter registration for an estimated 21 million Americans who lack a citizenship document on hand.

Current law (the “motor voter” law, NVRA 1993) lets Americans register by simply attesting under penalty of perjury that they are citizens — no document check required. The SAVE Act removes that self-attestation path for federal races.

The Government Accountability Office estimated in a 2024 study that roughly 21.3 million U.S. citizens of voting age do not readily have a passport or birth certificate accessible. States without Real ID citizenship markers on licenses — a list that still includes several large states — may require additional document collection from these registrants.

Proponents say it closes a genuine security gap. Critics say the documentation burden falls disproportionately on certain demographics. Both positions are part of the public record; the law itself is the fact on the ground.

3 How it impacts your rights

Three questions to answer before your next registration.

✓

Already registered and not moving? You’re fine. This only applies when you register for the first time or re-register at a new address in a new state.

✓

Moving or registering for the first time? Locate one of the following before you visit your county clerk or submit an online form: U.S. passport, U.S. birth certificate, or your state-issued Real ID (check with your DMV whether your state’s license includes a citizenship marker).

⚠

Don’t have a passport or birth certificate? Contact your state vital records office to request a certified birth certificate copy (typically $10–$35; most states process in 2–4 weeks). Do this before any registration deadline, not the day of.
