That benefit is short-term disability insurance. Here's the plain-English version of what it is, why it matters, and why it costs less than you think.
 

What short-term disability actually does

If an employee gets sick, has surgery, or has a baby and can't work for a few weeks or months, short-term disability replaces a percentage of their paycheck — typically 50–70% — so they don't have to drain their savings or come back before they're ready.

The employer doesn't pay the claim. The insurance carrier does. Your job is simply to offer it.

Quick fact

Most group short-term disability plans are voluntary — meaning employees pay the premium themselves through payroll deduction. Your cost to set it up? Often zero. You're the one who made it available.

Why this one lands in job interviews

Candidates — especially those with families — ask about it. When they do, most small employers say "we don't have that." Most large employers do. That's the gap short-term disability closes, quickly and cheaply.

Beyond hiring, it's a retention play. Employees who know they're protected financially during a serious illness or a new-baby leave come back to work. Ones who aren't protected often don't — or land in financial stress that shows up on the job.
