The wall between your personal and business credit (and why it's leaking)

A 3-minute read  ·  Glenn Grahl, All Point Consultants

Hi {{first_name}},

Every month I'm going to share one idea that actually moves the needle — no noise, no sales pitch, just the kind of clarity I wish more business owners had earlier.

This month's idea: why mixing your personal and business credit is silently capping your funding — and raising your risk at the same time.

 

Think of it like a firewall that has a hole in it

When you started your business, you were probably told to keep personal and business finances separate. Good advice. But here's what most people don't realize: your credit has the same problem, and it's far less visible.

Imagine your personal credit is a fuse box and your business credit is a separate circuit. When they're properly wired, a problem in one room doesn't blow out the whole house. But when the wiring is tangled — when your business is drawing on your personal credit, or when lenders can't tell the difference between you and your company — a single blown fuse can take everything down with it.

That's commingled credit. And it's more common than you'd think.

How it happens quietly

None of this happens overnight. It creeps in through ordinary decisions:

›

You used a personal card to cover a business expense — just once, then again, then regularly.
›

Your business loan required a personal guarantee — now your personal score takes the hit if the business stumbles.
›

Your business is a sole proprietorship or single-member LLC with no separate credit profile — so in lenders' eyes, there is no wall at all.
›

You applied for business financing and were told "we'll need to pull your personal credit" — a signal that your business hasn't built its own fundability yet.

None of these feel like mistakes in the moment. But each one makes the wall thinner — and riskier.

Two ways it caps your funding

First: if your personal score is carrying the weight of your business needs, you're limited by whatever your personal credit allows — not by what your business actually earns or owns. Lenders who would extend $150K to a properly structured business might only approve $25K when they're looking at your personal picture.

Second: when personal and business credit are entangled, a slow quarter or a single late business payment can damage your personal score — the one you need for a mortgage, a car, or a personal emergency. The risk flows both directions.

A clean separation means each profile grows independently — your business builds its own track record, and your personal finances stay protected.

 

This Month's Clarity Tip

Do a 5-minute wall check: Pull up your last 3 months of business expenses. How many were paid with a personal card or from a personal account? If it's more than zero, that's where the leak starts. The fix isn't complicated — it starts with consistent separation of accounts and building a dedicated business credit profile — but you have to know the hole is there first.

Even one step — opening a dedicated business checking account and routing all business revenue through it — changes how lenders read your picture over time.
