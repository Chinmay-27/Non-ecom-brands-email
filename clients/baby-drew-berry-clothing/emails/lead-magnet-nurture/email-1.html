Most baby clothing labels tell you the fiber content. What they don't tell you is how that fiber was processed — and for a newborn with skin that's 30% thinner than yours, that detail matters more than the cute pattern on the front.

Here's what parents often find out the hard way:

Harsh synthetics — polyester, acrylic blends — trap heat and don't breathe. They're common in fast-fashion baby clothes because they're cheap. They're also why some babies seem perpetually rashy and uncomfortable.

Fast-fashion cotton that pills after three washes. It starts soft enough to fool you at the store, then turns rough right around the time your baby is wearing it most — all day, every day.

The one label detail most parents miss

When you're shopping online and can't run the fabric through your fingers, the label is all you have. But most parents only check the fiber name — not the grade or certification next to it.

Bamboo viscose, for example, is genuinely breathable and naturally thermal-regulating. But the word "bamboo" on a tag without OEKO-TEX or a comparable certification tells you almost nothing about what chemicals touched that fiber during processing. Your guide covers exactly what to look for — and why bamboo viscose handled the right way is so different from standard cotton blends.
