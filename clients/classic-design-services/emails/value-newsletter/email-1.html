Protecting What Matters — Issue No. 1

3 mistakes that cost designers a client on installation day

Hi {{first_name}},

After handling thousands of white-glove projects across more than 100,000 sq ft of climate-controlled space, we've watched the same three moments derail what should have been perfect installations. Not shipping damage. Not traffic. Not the client changing her mind the week before.

Three specific, avoidable missteps — each one with a simple fix. Here's what we see, and how to protect yourself at every stage.
 

1

Signing the delivery receipt before you inspect

A freight carrier's driver is friendly, in a hurry, and counting on you to sign fast. The moment you do, you've legally accepted the shipment in whatever condition it arrived. Any damage discovered afterward — a cracked leg, a crushed corner, a missing crate panel — is now a claims fight you'll almost certainly lose.

The fix: Inspect every piece before signing. Note any external damage on the receipt — even writing "subject to inspection" preserves your rights — and photograph the crating condition before anything is removed. This 10-minute step is the difference between a clean insurance claim and an out-of-pocket repair conversation with your client.
 

2

Staging in an uncontrolled environment

A $14,000 antique settee sitting in a non-climate-controlled garage or standard staging warehouse for two weeks will almost certainly move. Wood expands, veneer lifts, leather dries, silk dims. The damage isn't dramatic at first — it's subtle. By installation day you're explaining to a client why the showroom piece looks different from what she approved six months ago.

The fix: Store anything made of natural materials — hardwood, leather, antique finishes, hand-painted silk — in a temperature- and humidity-controlled environment (65–75°F, 45–55% relative humidity). If your current receiving or staging arrangement doesn't meet that standard, every piece waiting there is at quiet, ongoing risk.
 

3

Rushing the uncrate on installation day

Installation day has a schedule. The client is there. You're running 20 minutes behind. The instinct is to move quickly. This is exactly when most on-site damage happens — a box knife through a lampshade, a corner dragged across a freshly laid wood floor, a fragile foot placed on an unpadded surface. Small, preventable moments that live in your professional memory forever.

The fix: Unpack in a designated clear zone away from finished surfaces. Keep original packing material until the piece is placed and approved. Build 15 extra minutes per major item into your install schedule. A calm uncrate is invisible to the client; a rushed one is a conversation you'll have with their insurance adjuster.
