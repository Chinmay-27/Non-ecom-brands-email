The real reason AI initiatives stall: it's not the model

Last quarter, a health system's technology leadership team brought in a large language model to automate prior-authorization workflows. The model was excellent. The pilot was promising. And then—six weeks before go-live—the project stalled.

The culprit wasn't the AI. It was that the EHR, the prior-auth platform, and the billing system each held a different version of the same patient record. When the model tried to pull a real-time data feed to make a decision, it got three conflicting answers. The team spent the next six weeks not tuning the AI—but manually reconciling data across systems that were never designed to talk to each other.

This pattern repeats in almost every healthcare AI rollout we see. The technology investment stalls at the data layer, not the model layer.

“We didn't have an AI problem. We had a data problem we'd been living with for years—the AI just made it impossible to ignore.”

— VP of Revenue Cycle, regional health system

Three operational failure modes (and what fixes them)

Based on what we see across healthcare and enterprise systems, AI initiatives stall for three predictable reasons:

1

Fragmented data with no single source of truth

When your EHR says one thing, your billing platform says another, and your ERP is silent, the AI has to choose which version of reality to act on. It can't. The fix isn't better AI—it's a semantic data layer that unifies records across systems in real time, giving every downstream application one authoritative answer.
2

Manual hand-offs that the AI can't bridge

Most healthcare workflows have a human-in-the-middle not by design but by necessity—because systems don't pass information cleanly to the next step. AI automation hits a wall wherever those hand-offs exist. Until the underlying workflow is connected end-to-end, the AI can only automate the parts on either side of the gap.
3

Governance built around the old stack

Access controls, audit trails, and data ownership policies were designed for a world where data lived in one system. When AI needs to read across five systems and write outputs back to three, legacy governance structures slow or block the implementation entirely—not for compliance reasons, but because nobody mapped the new data flows before go-live.

In Practice

What it looks like when two siloed systems actually talk

A mid-size IDN's supply chain and revenue cycle teams were running on separate platforms with no live connection. Every week, staff spent roughly 14 hours manually cross-referencing surgical supply costs in the ERP against the charge codes logged in the EHR—looking for billing discrepancies before claims went out.

We deployed a semantic interoperability layer between the two systems—not replacing either platform, but building a real-time bridge that mapped supply records to charge codes automatically. Within three weeks of go-live:

✓ The 14-hour weekly reconciliation process dropped to under 40 minutes—automated
✓ Billing accuracy on surgical cases improved by 23% in the first billing cycle
✓ Leadership now has a live dashboard that surfaces cost-vs-charge variance in real time—no spreadsheets

Neither legacy system was replaced. Both are now part of a unified, AI-ready data layer.

The takeaway

Before your next AI initiative goes into procurement, do one diagnostic: map every system that needs to read or write data for this workflow to function. If any two of those systems currently require a human hand-off to exchange information, you have an interoperability problem that will surface at go-live—not before.

The good news: it's solvable without a rip-and-replace. A semantic data layer sits between your existing platforms and translates data in real time. Your EHR doesn't need to know how your ERP speaks. The layer handles it.
