This Month's Teaching Tip

Soft foundation stops: the forgotten tool for chant accompaniment

One of the most common mistakes I hear in parish chant accompaniment is the organist pulling out too much registration — a full Principal chorus that overwhelms the choir and turns the congregation's attention to the organ rather than the melody. The antiphon gets buried.

The remedy is simple: begin with a single soft 8’ foundation stop — a Gedackt or a stopped Diapason if your instrument has one, or the quietest flute available on a digital organ. Add nothing else. Let the choir hear itself, and let the chant melody breathe.

On a Rodgers or Roland digital instrument, look for stops labeled Rohrflöte 8’, Bourdon 8’, or Flûte Harmonique 8’ and keep your swell box half-closed. The goal is support, not decoration. Once the choir is confident in the melody, you can consider adding a quiet 4’ flute — but more often than not, you won't need it.

Seasonal Repertoire

3 settings to rehearse before September

A handful of weeks of low-pressure summer rehearsal is the best gift you can give your choir for the fall. These three pieces reward early preparation and sit comfortably in most volunteer ensembles.

Ordinary Time / Corpus Christi

Panis Angelicus — Mode III Chant Setting

A universally beloved Eucharistic text that works equally well for Communion processions during late summer and the feast of Corpus Christi. The Mode III melody is straightforward enough for new choir members to learn quickly, and the repetitive phrase structure means it settles into the voice after two rehearsals. Pair it with a soft 8’ foundation stop and let the melody lead.

September — Our Lady's Month

Salve Regina — Solemn Tone

September brings the Nativity of Mary (Sept. 8) and opens the traditional season of Marian devotion before the October rosary. The Solemn Tone Salve Regina is among the most recognized pieces in the Catholic choral repertoire — and a new MIDI file is now available on the shelf to help you hear how the accompaniment should sit (see below). Even a choir that has sung it before benefits from reviewing the textual inflections at the closing O clemens phrase.

Feast of All Saints — November 1

Gaudeamus omnes — Introit for All Saints

November 1 arrives faster than it should, and the proper chant for All Saints is genuinely rewarding to sing. Gaudeamus omnes has an exultant opening interval that immediately reads as festive — congregations recognize it. Beginning rehearsal in late summer gives your schola time to internalize the psalm tone and attempt the introit verse without it feeling rushed. The sheet music is in the Free Music Shelf under “Propers for November.”
