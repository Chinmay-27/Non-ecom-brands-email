Before you open it, I want to name something directly. If you’ve stared at a page of Gregorian chant and felt a quiet wave of bewilderment — those four lines instead of five, the square noteheads, the diamond-shaped neumes, the clef that seems to belong to a different century — you are not alone, and you are not behind. Every parish musician I’ve ever taught has stood in exactly that same place.

Here is what I want you to know before we begin: chant notation is actually simpler than modern staff notation, not harder. There are no barlines counting out beats. There is no time signature telling you how to subdivide. The notation exists for one purpose only — to carry the melody of the text. Once you see it that way, the intimidation evaporates. This week, I’ll prove it.

Your 14-Week Roadmap

One lesson a week is all it takes. Fourteen weeks from today, before the next liturgical season turns, you will have everything you need to lead your choir or congregation in Gregorian chant. Here is exactly where we are going:

Weeks 1–2 Reading the staff. The four-line staff, clefs (do and fa), and your first neumes.
Weeks 3–4 Rhythm and flow. How chant rhythm follows the Latin text, not a metronome.
Weeks 5–7 The eight modes. Understanding the modal system so every chant finally makes musical sense.
Weeks 8–10 The Mass Ordinary. Learning the chant settings your congregation will actually sing at Mass.
Weeks 11–12 The Mass Propers. Introit, Offertory, Communion antiphons — the heart of the liturgical repertoire.
Weeks 13–14 Leading your choir. Practical conducting, rehearsal technique, and preparing for your first Mass.

Each week I will send you a short note before the next lesson arrives — a reminder, a small encouragement, sometimes a question to think about. There is no quiz, no homework, and absolutely no pressure. This is a choir loft, not a classroom. The only requirement is that you keep showing up.
