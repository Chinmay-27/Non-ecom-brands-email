This Month’s Tip

Your grind size is probably wrong for your brewer.

Fresh coffee is the foundation — that part we’ve got handled. But freshness alone won’t save an extraction that’s fighting your brew method. Grind size is the most underrated variable in home brewing, and the fix takes about ten seconds.

Here’s why it matters: grind size controls how fast water moves through the grounds and how much it extracts. Go too fine for your method and you get over-extraction — bitter, sharp, hollow in the finish. Go too coarse and the water blows straight through — weak, sour, flat. Neither one is the coffee’s fault.

Most pre-ground bags at the grocery store are set for a generic drip machine. If you’re using a pour-over, an AeroPress, or a French press — you’re already at a disadvantage before the water hits the kettle.

Quick Reference

Pour-over  (V60, Chemex, Kalita)

Medium-fine — like table salt. If it tastes bitter, go one notch coarser.

AeroPress

Medium — versatile by design, but medium gives the cleanest, most balanced cup for a 2-minute steep.

French Press

Coarse — like rough sea salt. Fine grinds slip through the mesh filter and make the cup muddy.

Drip machine

Medium — like dry sand. Most pre-ground bags are set here, so at least you’re not fighting the method.

Espresso

Fine — like powdered sugar, but not quite flour. Dial in by shot time: aim for 25–30 seconds start to finish.

The fastest way to test this: brew two cups back to back, same coffee, one grind a couple notches coarser than your current setting. You’ll taste the difference right away — and that’s exactly what fresh coffee makes obvious that stale coffee hides.

Ethiopia is where Arabica coffee began, and Yirgacheffe is where it still shows you what it can do at its best. The Gedeo Zone sits at roughly 6,000 feet above sea level — high enough that the beans develop slowly, concentrating the sugars that become brightness and floral complexity in the cup.

This lot is washed-process, meaning the fruit cherry is stripped away before drying. That scrubs the flavor back to the seed itself — no fermented or wine-like notes, just the clean, tea-like clarity that Yirgacheffe is known for worldwide. Floral on the nose, bright on the palate, and long in the finish.

It arrived here days after roasting. For a light roast, that matters — the jasmine and bergamot notes you smell on the bag fade quickly in stale coffee. Brew it as a pour-over on medium-fine (see above), and you’ll get exactly what it’s meant to be.
