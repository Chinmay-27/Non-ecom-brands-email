01 — The Story

The Wythe, and why it changed everything.

Wythe Ave — Williamsburg, Brooklyn

Wythe Avenue used to be a street of cooperage warehouses. People made barrels there — white oak staved, iron-hooped, the craft unchanged for two hundred years. By the time we moved our bench into a second-floor studio off Wythe in 2011, the warehouses had been converted into event spaces and boutiques, but you could still read the bones of the buildings. The proportions. The weight of things made to last.

The Wythe frame came out of that first year on the street. We'd been pushing ourselves to use Italian acetate in its thickest form — 6mm — because we wanted a frame that carried visible heft without feeling heavy on the face. The goal was the same as the coopers': something you'd notice was well-made without being told it was well-made.

The Wythe is still in the collection. It's one of three frames we've never discontinued. When people ask us what to get if they're only going to own one pair, we usually say: start there.

02 — The Tip

Oval face? Stop defaulting to rounds.

The received wisdom — "oval faces can wear anything" — is technically true and practically useless. It tells you nothing about what actually works. What we've noticed, after a few thousand in-store fittings, is this: oval faces often look their sharpest in a frame with a flat or geometric top edge. Something with a slight browline, a square upper rim, or a keystone bridge.

The reason is contrast. A round frame on an oval face softens everything — it can read as bland rather than refined. Add a horizontal line at the top and the eye has something to land on. The face gains structure without losing its ease.

"Frame choice is about contrast, not compatibility. The face you have is the starting point — the frame should do something interesting with it."

— From the bench, Williamsburg studio

03 — On the Bench

The Flushing — now available in Honey Tortoise.

New colorway — June 2026

Honey Tortoise

We've been working on the Flushing colorway since late winter. The original Flushing is our most versatile silhouette — a wide, slightly flattened oval — and the existing colors (Matte Black, Raw Tortoise) sell to people who want the frame to disappear. Honey Tortoise is for the other kind of person: the one who wants you to notice.

It's a warm amber acetate with a deep brown core that shifts in light. We pulled the same Italian stock we use for the Wythe. In person it reads almost like a piece of polished wood. Online, it just reads like a very good frame.

It'll be live in the shop and on the site later this month. If you'd like to know the moment it drops, just reply to this email — we'll send you a note before it goes on the site.
