The craft behind your pair.

Most frames you find at a chain are injection-molded, shipped from overseas, and assembled in minutes. Yours aren't. This is what's actually happening to them before they reach you.

1

Italian acetate, cut by hand

Your sheet of Mazzucchelli acetate — the same material used by the heritage European houses — is cut from a solid block, not poured into a mold. The color and pattern run all the way through.
2

Real metal hinges, fitted by hand

Barrel hinges are pressed and set individually — the reason your frames will still open and close cleanly in year three. No plastic pivots, no rivets that loosen in six months.
3

Hand-tumbled to a polish

Each frame goes through a multi-stage tumbling and polishing process before it leaves the studio. No sharp edges, no manufacturing artifacts — just a surface that gets better-looking the more you handle it.

3 rules to make them last

Always use two hands. Acetate frames are strong, but pulling one arm open repeatedly with one hand torques the frame — the same way a door hinge wears out faster if you push from the wrong edge.

Store them in the case. Heat is acetate's enemy — a car dashboard in summer can warp a frame in under an hour. When you're not wearing them, the hard case keeps them safe.

Clean with the cloth, not your shirt. Microfiber is cut for optics; a cotton tee, even a soft one, will eventually micro-scratch the lens coating.
