This issue's pup-nutrition tip

The 3 words hiding on most “healthy” treat labels

Hey {{first_name}},

Grab the nearest bag of dog treats you own — even ours, if you have it. Flip it over. Now read the ingredient list out loud.

How far did you get before you hit a word you couldn't pronounce?

For most bags on the shelf, it's word number three or four. That's not an accident — and it's not something your dog can opt out of.

In this issue, I'm going to show you the three specific words (actually, word types) that marketers use to dress up cheap ingredients on a "natural" label, why they matter for your pup, and how to spot them in about 30 seconds flat.

Word Type #1

"By-product" — the protein that isn't really protein

A label that says chicken by-product meal sounds a lot like chicken. It isn't. By-product meal is a rendered mix of beaks, feet, undeveloped eggs, and internal organs — whatever's left after the parts humans actually eat are removed.

It's not toxic. But it's also not what you picture when you see a smiling golden retriever on the packaging. The quality and digestibility vary wildly batch to batch, and dogs with sensitive stomachs often react to it without their owners ever tracing the cause.

Word Type #2

"Digest" — the filler hiding in plain sight

Look for anything ending in "digest" — beef digest, liver digest, animal digest. These are chemically or enzymatically processed animal tissues used primarily as flavoring agents, often sprayed onto the outside of kibble or mixed into soft treats to make a cheap base taste appealing.

The word sounds almost healthy — your body digests food, right? — but on a label it means something entirely different. It's bulk and palatability on the cheap, not nutrition.

Word Type #3

"-ate" and "-ite" endings — the preservative parade

BHA, BHT, ethoxyquin, sodium nitrite, potassium sorbate. These are the shelf-life extenders. Some are under ongoing scrutiny in canine nutrition research. Others are simply unnecessary in a treat that's made fresh and sold quickly.

A clean treat with a short ingredient list doesn't need them. When you see a long chemical tail on an ingredient list, that's the brand telling you their base ingredients aren't good enough to stand on their own — they need chemistry to stay shelf-stable for 18 months.

The 30-second label scan

Three questions to ask before the treat hits the cart:

1.

How many ingredients? Under five is a green flag. Over fifteen, read everything.
2.

What's listed first? Ingredients are listed by weight — if the first ingredient isn't a recognizable whole food, that's the bulk of what's in the bag.
3.

Can you say every word out loud? If something sounds like a chemistry lab, it is one. The more you recognize, the better.

The shortcut that bypasses all of the above

When we started making treats out of our Boston kitchen, we made a rule for ourselves: if you can't read the whole ingredient list in one breath, we're not making it.

That's why everything we make is one ingredient. Just Chicken. Just Beef. Just Turkey. Just Fish Skins. No by-products, no digest, no chemical preservatives — because a single whole ingredient doesn't need any of that.

Customers tell us their dogs' coats look better, their digestion settled down, and their sensitive-stomach pups finally have a treat that agrees with them. We can't make medical claims and we won't — but we can tell you that "just one ingredient" leaves no room for mystery.

This issue's pup-nutrition tip

Ingredient splitting: the oldest trick in the book

Brands sometimes list the same filler ingredient under multiple names to push it lower on the list. You'll see ground corn, corn flour, corn gluten meal — three separate listings that, combined, would rank higher than the protein. Add them up mentally. If corn (or rice, or potato, in any form) would outweigh the named meat, that's a filler-first product wearing a protein costume.
