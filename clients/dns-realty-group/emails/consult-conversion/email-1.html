Your online estimate vs. what your home would actually sell for

You got a number. Zillow showed you a figure, or Redfin gave you a range — and it’s sitting there in the back of your mind. Maybe it felt higher than you expected. Maybe lower. Either way, you’re probably wondering: is that actually what I could get?

Here’s the honest answer from someone who works this market every day: automated estimates in Southern Maryland and the DC metro can swing by $30,000 to $80,000 — in either direction. That gap is not a rounding error. It’s the difference between a clean sale and leaving real money on the table.

 

Why the algorithm gets it wrong

Online tools pull broad data — county records, regional trends, ZIP-code averages. What they can’t see:

🏚 Your street’s recent comps Not the ZIP code average
🔨 Your home’s condition & upgrades Kitchen, roof, HVAC — real dollars
📈 Current buyer demand this week Not last quarter’s trend

A Comparative Market Analysis — a real CMA done by a local agent — pulls actual closed sales within a half-mile of your address from the last 60 to 90 days, adjusts for your home’s specific features, and layers in where buyer demand is right now. That’s the number you can actually take to the closing table.
