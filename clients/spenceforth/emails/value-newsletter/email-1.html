This Week's Idea

The difference between writing for an audience and writing for yourself — and why the best posts do both

There's a piece I keep coming back to: the moment when a post stops being a draft you're trying to get right and becomes something you actually want someone to read. It's a specific feeling, and I think I finally understand what causes it.

The short version: writing for an audience alone makes you hedge. You soften the edges, you add qualifiers, you explain things you already know — and the post gets safer but somehow smaller. Writing only for yourself means the thing that makes it interesting to you stays private, invisible, assumed.

The posts that land — the ones people forward, the ones that collect replies days later — tend to solve that tension a specific way. They're written from genuine curiosity, but they translated for someone standing slightly outside the writer's head. Not dumbed down. Translated.

That's what this week's post is about. I got into the practical side of it — what translation actually looks like at the sentence level — and I think it's one of the more useful things I've written.

I've been running a small experiment lately: before I publish anything, I write one sentence that starts with "This is worth your time because…" and I have to finish it honestly. It sounds obvious. It's not. The number of drafts that quietly died when I couldn't complete that sentence is embarrassing.
