The HALT Fentanyl Act has gained bipartisan support from lawmakers on both sides of the aisle — not because of lobbyists, but because families testified. Parents walked into Senate offices carrying photos of their children. They said the names out loud. They made it impossible to look away.

That is the only thing that has ever moved this issue forward. Not party platforms. Not political strategy. Families refusing to be silent.

 

A family's story

"I told them his name. I told them he was 22. And I watched a senator's aide write it down."

"After Ryan died, I didn't know what to do with myself. I couldn't sleep. I kept thinking: how many other kids is this going to take? Someone told me about United Against Fentanyl. I went to Washington and I testified. I don't know if I changed anything that day — but I felt like I was doing something. And then I found out our representative co-sponsored the bill two months later."

— Lisa M., mother of Ryan, 22

Consented network spokesperson

Lisa is not a lobbyist. She is not a politician. She is a mother who lost her son and decided that his name would be heard in the rooms where decisions are made.

Fentanyl is not a left vs. right issue. It is a human issue. It has taken children from Republican families in rural counties and Democratic families in coastal cities. The only thing that makes lawmakers act — across both parties — is knowing that the people who vote for them demand it.
