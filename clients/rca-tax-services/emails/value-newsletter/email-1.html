Ray’s Strategy of the Month

The deduction most $500K+ earners never claim — and how to fix it

Hi {{first_name}},

This month I want to walk you through one strategy that I consistently see high earners miss — even people who already have a good CPA. It’s not exotic, it’s not a loophole, and the IRS has blessed it for decades. But it requires proactive setup, which is exactly why it slips through the cracks.

 

The Defined Benefit Plan + S-Corp combination

If you run a business, practice, or consultancy as an S-Corporation — or if you’re eligible to elect S-Corp status — and you’re over 45 with substantial income, this combination may be the single highest-leverage tax move available to you right now.

Here’s how it works in plain English.

1

The S-Corp baseline: cut self-employment tax first

A properly structured S-Corp lets you split your business income into a reasonable W-2 salary and a distribution. You pay self-employment tax (15.3%) only on the salary — not on the distribution. On $600K of net income, with a $175K salary, that alone can save $32,000–$45,000 per year depending on your structure. Most business owners in this range either haven’t made the election or have set their salary too high.

2

Layer in a Defined Benefit plan: shelter income a 401(k) can’t touch

A SEP-IRA maxes out around $70,000. A Solo 401(k) caps around $70,000. These are fine — but if you’re 50+ with high income, a Defined Benefit plan can allow contributions of $150,000 to $280,000+ annually, depending on your age and income. Those contributions are a dollar-for-dollar deduction. Pair it with the S-Corp and you’re running two engines simultaneously.

3

The combined effect at 37%

Say you’re a 54-year-old physician with a medical practice generating $850,000 in net income. You elect S-Corp status with a $200,000 W-2 salary. You establish a Defined Benefit plan with an actuarially-calculated contribution of $240,000. Between the FICA savings on the distribution and the DB deduction, you may be looking at well over $100,000 in combined annual tax savings — year after year, compounding inside a protected retirement account.

By the numbers

S-Corp FICA savings (on $650K distribution) ~$36,400
DB plan deduction at 37% marginal rate ($240K contribution) ~$88,800
Combined annual tax reduction ~$125,200

Why doesn’t your CPA bring this up?

Setting up a Defined Benefit plan requires actuarial calculations, IRS plan documents, and coordination with your entity structure. It’s not a checkbox on a tax return — it’s forward planning that has to happen before year-end. Most filing-focused CPAs see it as scope creep. For us, it’s a standard conversation in Q3.

There are also requirements: the plan must be established before December 31st of the tax year you want to claim it, and the math only makes sense at a certain income threshold. If you wait until your CPA mentions it in April, you’ve already missed the window.
