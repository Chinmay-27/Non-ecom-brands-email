The Question Every Believer Is Asking

Is AI spiritually dangerous?

Let’s not dodge it.
Let’s settle it — with Scripture.

{{first_name}}, I need to say something that most Christian voices in the AI conversation won’t say plainly:

Your fear is not irrational. And your curiosity isn’t sinful.

You’ve been warned that AI will replace pastors, corrupt theology, breed spiritual laziness, and maybe usher in something darker. Some of those warnings come from sincere, Spirit-filled believers. They deserve a real answer — not a dismissal and not a sales pitch.

So here’s the framework I keep coming back to. It’s not my framework. It’s the one the Bible already gives us.

“Whatever you do, do it all for the glory of God.”

1 Corinthians 10:31

Notice what Paul doesn’t say. He doesn’t hand you a list of approved tools. He hands you a governing question: Who are you serving with it?

That’s the only question that matters when it comes to AI. Not what AI is — but who you become when you use it.

The Two Paths

   

AI as Idol

You hand AI the questions only God should answer. You let it replace the still, small voice. You skip the prayer because the prompt was faster. You lean on its confidence the way you used to lean on His Word. That is spiritually dangerous — not because AI is demonic, but because you’ve stopped seeking the One who is divine.
   

AI as Tool

You use it to study Scripture more deeply. To reach more people with the Gospel. To free up hours of administration so you can actually pastor, parent, and pray. You stay the steward; the Holy Spirit stays the source. That is faithful innovation — the kind that has always moved the Kingdom forward.

The printing press was called spiritually dangerous. The radio was called spiritually dangerous. Every generation of believers has had to decide: do we steward this or fear it?

The ones who stewarded it reshaped history for the Kingdom. The ones who only feared it watched from the sidelines while the world moved on.

“Do not be conformed to this world, but be transformed by the renewal of your mind, that by testing you may discern what is the will of God.”

Romans 12:2

Transformed minds test what is good. That’s not a warning away from AI — it’s an invitation to engage it with discernment instead of paralysis.

But here’s where the real tension lives for many of you — especially if you’re in ministry, preaching, studying theology, or leading a church:

What happens when AI can produce a sermon in 30 seconds? What happens when it can answer doctrinal questions faster than any seminary-trained pastor? What does that mean for your calling — and for the theology you’ve spent a lifetime building?

That’s the question at the heart of a book I want to put in your hands today.
