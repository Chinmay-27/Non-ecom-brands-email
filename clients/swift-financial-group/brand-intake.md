# Brand Intake — Swift Financial Group, LLC
**URL:** https://swiftfinancialgroup.net
**Date:** 2026-06-04

---

## 1. The Offer

**What does the brand actually sell?**
Swift Financial Group is a note-buying company. Their core service is purchasing seller-financed (owner-financed) mortgage notes from individuals who hold them. They also offer partial note purchases — the note holder can sell a predetermined number of future payments for a lump sum, then reclaim the note later.

**Core outcome the customer is buying:**
Immediate, full liquidity. The note holder stops waiting years for small monthly payments and receives all (or a significant portion) of their money today as a single lump sum. Peace of mind replaces payment-management anxiety.

**Price point & commitment:**
- One-time transaction; no ongoing fees or subscriptions.
- The seller pays nothing — Swift covers all closing costs (title, notary, etc.).
- Swift purchases notes at a market discount (typically 70–90 cents on the dollar, industry standard). Transaction values range from tens of thousands to several hundred thousand dollars depending on note balance.

**Vertical:**
B2B/Professional — Financial Services (note buying / private mortgage brokerage)

## 2. The Audience (Who They Serve)

**Primary ICP:**
Individuals currently holding a seller-financed mortgage note — people who sold a home or property without a bank and are now receiving monthly installment payments from the buyer. Typically: retirees, small landlords, and property investors who want liquidity rather than a trickle of payments.

**Secondary segments worth separating:**
1. **Retirees / estate planners** — financed a home sale years ago and now want a lump sum for living expenses, medical costs, or clean estate distribution.
2. **Active real estate investors / landlords** — regularly create owner-financed notes and sell them as part of their investment strategy; repeat potential.
3. **Heirs / inheritors** — received a mortgage note in an estate and have no desire to be a lender; most motivated by simplicity.
4. **One-time sellers who now regret waiting** — sold a home with owner financing for the first time; experiencing payment management fatigue or borrower risk concerns.

**Problems they're in when they start looking:**
- Fear that their buyer will default and they'll lose both the payments and the property
- Urgent cash need (medical emergency, tuition, investment opportunity, divorce settlement)
- Exhaustion from managing payment collection and borrower communication
- Realization that waiting 20–30 years to collect is not what they signed up for

**Where leads come from:**
- Direct web search ("sell my mortgage note," "note buyers")
- Referrals from real estate attorneys, CPAs, title companies, and real estate agents who facilitate owner-financed deals
- Word of mouth from previous clients (20+ years in business)

**How they describe success in their own words:**
"I have all my money now." "I don't have to worry about them stopping payments." "The process was simple and they handled everything."

## 3. The Sales Cycle

**Time from first contact → paying customer:**
Weeks to ~45 days. The quote can come same-day or within a few days; due diligence and closing typically takes 30–45 days after the offer is accepted.

**Is there a free trial, free consult, or first-session offer?**
Yes — a free, no-obligation quote. The note holder submits basic note details (balance, payment amount, property type, payment history) and receives a cash offer with no commitment required.

**Who/what closes the deal?**
The quote + a guided, transparent process. There is no evidence of a dedicated sales team; the close appears to be driven by the offer itself plus trust signals (20+ years in business, all costs covered, flexible closing options). The seller accepts or declines; if they accept, they submit paperwork.

**#1 objection or hesitation before buying:**
"I'll lose too much money selling at a discount." Note holders compare the lump sum to the total of all future payments and feel like they're giving money away. The real objection is a misunderstanding of time value of money and risk — the discount reflects interest and the risk Swift absorbs. Education here is the key unlock.

---

## 4. The "Aha" / First-Value Moment

**The activation event:**
Receiving the lump-sum funds at closing — the moment the note holder has all their money in hand, often after years of uncertainty and monthly trickles.

**Pre-close micro-aha:**
Accepting the offer and discovering the process truly requires minimal effort from them — no closing costs, flexible closing location, and a clear step-by-step guide. The relief of simplicity is itself a value moment.

**Self-serve or assisted?**
Assisted — Swift handles all due diligence, paperwork coordination, and closing logistics. The seller's only job is to accept the offer and sign documents.

## 5. Retention & Recurring Revenue

**Is revenue recurring?**
No — this is primarily a one-time transaction model. Revenue is transactional per note purchased.

**Repeat/renewal cycle:**
Not applicable for most clients. Exceptions: real estate investors with multiple notes (repeat buyers); clients who did a partial note sale and later return to sell the remainder.

**What does "expansion" look like?**
- Referrals: highest-value expansion channel. One satisfied note holder likely knows others (neighbors who also did owner financing, estate attorneys handling similar estates, real estate investors in their network).
- Partial → full: A client who sold partial payments may return later to sell the full remaining note.

**Churn / drop-off problem:**
The main drop-off risk is between quote issuance and close — leads who receive an offer but go cold (shop competitors, get cold feet about the discount, or simply get distracted). No ongoing churn risk post-transaction, since the relationship ends at close.

## 8. Goals & Constraints

**Primary goal:** More conversions — getting quote requesters to accept the offer and close.

**Target metric:** Quote-to-close conversion rate (baseline unknown).

**Brand voice / tone:**
Professional, plain-spoken, and reassuring. The audience is making a major financial decision and may feel vulnerable or uncertain. Copy should be:
- Clear over clever (explain the process in simple steps)
- Authoritative but not corporate (20+ years of experience earns trust)
- Warm and low-pressure (they are buying a service, not being sold to)
- Focus on the seller's peace of mind and freedom, not financial jargon

**Compliance constraints:**
Financial services — CAN-SPAM required (unsubscribe, physical address). Standard financial disclosure language in promotional copy ("offers vary based on note terms," "not a guarantee"). No HIPAA. Avoid language that implies guaranteed rates or specific returns.

**Sending volume:** Unknown; likely a smaller, highly targeted list given the niche.

---

## Output: First 3 Campaigns to Build

Based on the gaps above, the priority order is:

1. **Welcome & Onboarding** — New quote requesters need an immediate sequence that explains the process, builds trust, and addresses the discount objection before they go cold. This is the single biggest conversion lever.
2. **Trial/Consult Conversion (Quote Follow-Up)** — A dedicated sequence for leads who received a quote but haven't responded in 3–7 days. Overcome the discount objection, reinforce simplicity, create light urgency.
3. **Win-Back / Re-Engagement** — People who got a quote and didn't move forward are the warmest cold audience. A periodic re-engagement (every 3–6 months) addressing changing life circumstances (retirement, health events, market changes) can reactivate dormant leads at very low cost.

*(Referral campaign is a close 4th once the above are generating satisfied clients.)*
